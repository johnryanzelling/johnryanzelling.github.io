Unlocking the Pythagorean Theorem: A Flipped Escape-Room Lesson

This page is teacher-facing. It contains answers, worked solutions, facilitation moves, and suggested hints for the in-class activity.

Back to lesson

Complete 60-Minute Facilitation Plan

  1. 0-5 minutes: individual readiness problem.
  2. 5-12 minutes: targeted review.
  3. 12-17 minutes: group assignments and directions.
  4. 17-47 minutes: collaborative escape room.
  5. 47-54 minutes: solution review and error discussion.
  6. 54-58 minutes: individual exit ticket.
  7. 58-60 minutes: student survey.

Grouping Guidance

  • Use groups of three or four so every student has a role.
  • Assign Reader, Recorder, Checker, and Reporter. In a group of three, combine Reader and Reporter.
  • Place students who missed pre-class work near the catch-up station before joining a group.
  • Ask the Checker to verify that every answer includes reasoning, not just a code.

Worked Solutions

Lock 3

  • 32 + 42 = c2; 9 + 16 = 25; c = 5.
  • 62 + 82 = c2; 36 + 64 = 100; c = 10.
  • 52 + 122 = c2; 25 + 144 = 169; c = 13.

Lock 4

  • 6, 8, 10: 36 + 64 = 100 and 102 = 100, so yes.
  • 5, 6, 8: 25 + 36 = 61 and 82 = 64, so no.
  • 9, 12, 15: 81 + 144 = 225 and 152 = 225, so yes.
  • 8, 10, 12: 64 + 100 = 164 and 122 = 144, so no.

Final Challenge

  • Ladder: 62 + 82 = 36 + 64 = 100, so the ladder is 10 feet.
  • Screen: 92 + 122 = 81 + 144 = 225, so the diagonal is 15 inches.
  • Field: 52 + 122 = 25 + 144 = 169, so the shortcut is 13 units.

Completion Code

Use the three final challenge answers in order: 10-15-13.

Exit Ticket

  • 82 + 152 = 64 + 225 = 289, so c = 17.
  • Side c is the hypotenuse, located opposite the right angle and the longest side of the right triangle.
  • 72 + 102 = 49 + 100 = 149. 122 = 144. 149 ≠ 144, so the sides do not form a right triangle.
  • Accept important steps such as identifying the hypotenuse, squaring both legs, adding square areas, comparing c2, or taking the positive square root.

Exit-Ticket Written Response Guidance

  • Item 2: Look for the idea that side c is the hypotenuse, opposite the right angle, and the longest side. Students may use their own words.
  • Item 4: Accept any mathematically important step, especially identifying the hypotenuse, substituting legs correctly, squaring before adding, comparing both sides, or taking the positive square root.
  • Use written responses to separate vocabulary gaps from calculation gaps.

Common Misconceptions

  • Students may assume the hypotenuse is always slanted. Point them back to the side opposite the right angle.
  • Students may add side lengths instead of square areas. Ask, "What does the exponent 2 tell us to do first?"
  • Students may forget to take the square root after finding c2.
  • Students may choose yes or no for a right-triangle check without comparing both sides of the equation.
  • Students may treat the longest given side as c without checking that it is opposite the right angle or the largest side in a side-length set.
  • Students may answer 23 for the 8-15 triangle by adding the legs instead of using square areas.
  • Students may think 149 is close enough to 144 and incorrectly accept 7, 10, and 12 as a right triangle.

Suggested Next-Day Follow-Up

  • If many students miss item 1, reteach the difference between c2 and c using perfect-square cards.
  • If many students miss item 2, begin with a two-minute diagram sort focused only on identifying the hypotenuse.
  • If many students miss item 3, model a supported comparison table for a2 + b2 and c2.
  • If written responses are vague, use sentence frames before the next problem-solving task.

Targeted Teacher Questions

  • Which side is across from the right-angle marker?
  • What square area belongs to this side length?
  • What does c2 represent, and what does c represent?
  • How do you know your yes-or-no decision is supported?
  • Where do you see the right triangle in the real-world context?

Suggested Hints

  • Lock 1: Put your finger on the right-angle marker, then look across the triangle.
  • Lock 2: Squaring goes from side length to area; square root goes from area to side length.
  • Lock 3: Read your work in order: substitute, square, add, square root.
  • Lock 4: A decision needs two totals: a2 + b2 and c2.
  • Final challenge: Draw or imagine the right triangle before calculating.

Five-Minute Catch-Up Station

  1. Read: In a right triangle, the square areas on the legs add to the square area on the hypotenuse: a2 + b2 = c2.
  2. Label one right triangle by circling the right angle, naming the legs, and identifying the hypotenuse.
  3. Review the 3-4-5 example: 32 + 42 = 9 + 16 = 25, so c = 5.
  4. Answer one readiness question: If a square has area 81, what is the side length?
  5. Join a group and take a role.

Do not replay the full lecture for the entire class. Use this station for students who need the prerequisite bridge.

Extension Activity for Early Finishers

Ask early finishers to create one new no-calculator Pythagorean clue using a familiar triple. They must include a diagram or context, the worked solution, and one hint that helps without giving away the answer.
